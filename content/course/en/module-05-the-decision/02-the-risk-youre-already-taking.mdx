# The Risk You're Already Taking

## Every day you don't decide, you ARE deciding

I want you to try something.

Calculate the cost of your indecision. Right now. Not as a thought experiment. With actual numbers.

Take your dominant constraint from Module 4. Now answer:

- **How many months have you been thinking about this?**
- **What has that time cost you?** (In dollars, in missed opportunities, in health, in relationships.)

When Maria did this for her career, here's what she found:

- 26 months of thinking about leaving
- At her old salary of $4,200 vs the $6,800 she's earning now: **$67,600 in lost income**
- 5 nights per week of insomnia for 18 months
- Roughly $2,100 spent on therapy partially driven by work stress

She didn't calculate this until after the change. When she saw the number — $67,600 — she sat there for a while.

"I thought not deciding was the safe option," she said. "I didn't realize I was already paying for it."

## The two types of risk

Every time you face a decision, there are two risks. Most people only see one.

**Risk A: The risk of deciding wrong.**

You make the move. It doesn't work out. You lose money, time, status, comfort.

This risk is visible. Loud. Your brain is very good at imagining it. "What if I apply and get rejected? What if I leave and the new job is worse? What if I have the conversation and it ruins the relationship?"

**Risk B: The risk of not deciding at all.**

Nothing happens. You stay. The discomfort continues. The opportunity window slowly closes.

This risk is invisible. Quiet. Your brain is terrible at calculating it because nothing HAPPENED. There's no event to point at. Just the slow erosion of months becoming years.

**Risk B is almost always more expensive than Risk A.**

Because a wrong decision is fixable. You can learn from it. Recover. Try again.

A non-decision is permanent. Those months don't come back. The compounding cost is invisible, so you never notice it until you look back and count — like Maria did — and realize it cost you $67,600 and 18 months of bad sleep.

## Asymmetric risk

There's a concept that makes decisions much easier once you see it.

Most meaningful decisions have asymmetric risk:

- **The downside is survivable.** Maria applies and gets rejected? She still has her current job. She's no worse off than before.
- **The upside is transformational.** Maria gets the job? $2,600/month more, better health, better relationship, a completely different Tuesday.

Survivable downside. Transformational upside.

That's asymmetric risk. And it applies to most of the decisions you've been avoiding:

| The decision | The downside (survivable) | The upside (transformational) |
|---|---|---|
| Apply to new jobs | Get rejected, stay where you are | Get the offer, change your income and your Tuesday |
| Have the conversation | It's awkward for a week | The relationship either heals or you stop wasting years |
| Start the business | Lose some money, learn what doesn't work | Build the thing you've been dreaming about |
| Move to a new city | Feel lonely for 3 months | Design the life you actually want |

*Look at your decision. What's the realistic downside? Is it survivable? What's the realistic upside? Is it transformational?*

If the downside is survivable and the upside is transformational, you're not taking a risk by deciding.

**You're taking a risk by NOT deciding.**

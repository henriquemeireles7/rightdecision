# One at a Time

## Why not fix everything at once?

Because you're not the same person after fixing one thing.

This sounds abstract, so let me make it concrete.

Maria's state map had five problems: career (3/10), finances (3/10), health (4/10), relationship (5/10), emotional (3/10). Five fires. The temptation is to fight them all at once.

New job AND fix the relationship AND start exercising AND save money AND work on emotional health.

That's not a plan. That's a New Year's resolution list. And you know how those end.

Here's what actually happened when Maria focused on one thing — her career:

**Month 1:** She updated her resume. Applied to three companies. Got two interviews.

**Month 2:** She got an offer. $6,800/month, up from $4,200. She accepted.

**Month 3:** With the new salary, her finances went from 3/10 to 6/10 without even trying. She just had more money. The financial "problem" partially solved itself.

**Month 4:** With a job she actually liked, the Sunday dread disappeared. The insomnia stopped. Her health went from 4/10 to 6/10. She had energy to exercise again.

**Month 5:** With more energy and less resentment about work, she was genuinely nicer to Carlos. Relationship went from 5/10 to 7/10.

One constraint resolved. Four domains improved.

**That's why you focus on one at a time.** The right one pulls everything else up with it.

## The person who solves problem A is not the same person who faces problem B

This is the part most people miss.

Maria before the job change: exhausted, resentful, $800 in savings, dreading Mondays, no energy for Carlos, no energy for the gym.

Maria after the job change: energized, earning more, sleeping well, actually present with Carlos.

The Maria who now looks at her relationship is a completely different person than the Maria who was trying to "fix things with Carlos" while exhausted and resentful.

The same problem. A different person approaching it. Completely different results.

**Sequential, not parallel.** Not because the other problems don't matter. Because YOU change when you resolve the first one. And the changed you handles the next problem better.

## When two constraints seem equal

Sometimes you list everything, rate it, and two constraints score the same.

Career: 9. Finances: 9.

What do you do?

**Pick the one that makes the other easier.**

If fixing your career would also fix your finances (higher salary), career is the primary. Finances waits.

If fixing your finances would free you to take a risk on career (savings as safety net), finances is the primary. Career waits.

The tie-breaker is always: **which one, if resolved, makes the other one smaller?**

## When constraints seem entangled

"I can't leave my job because I need the money, and I can't save money because I'm stressed about my job so I spend it on comfort."

Feels like one big problem. It's not.

It's two problems pretending to be one.

Pick one. The entanglement loosens when you pull on one thread. You don't need to untangle the whole knot. You just need to find the thread that, when pulled, loosens everything else.

For the example above: the career is probably the thread. A better job solves both the income and the stress-spending.

*Which thread, if you pulled it, would loosen your knot?*

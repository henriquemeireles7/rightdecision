# Is This Yours?

## I chased my mother's dream for eight years

I spent my twenties building the career my mother wanted me to have. Good company. Good title. Good salary. The kind of job you can explain at a family dinner and everyone nods.

I was miserable.

Not because the job was bad. It wasn't. The company was fine. The salary was fine. The title was fine.

But it was her fine, not mine.

I didn't want to manage people. I wanted to build things. I didn't want stability. I wanted freedom. I didn't want a title that impressed strangers. I wanted work that excited me on a Tuesday afternoon.

It took me eight years to realize I was climbing someone else's mountain. And when I got close to the top, I felt nothing. Not relief. Not pride. Nothing.

Because it wasn't mine.

## Where borrowed dreams come from

The most dangerous target state is one that belongs to someone else. You can spend years chasing it. You can even catch it. And when you arrive, you feel empty.

Not because success is empty. Because it wasn't YOUR success.

Borrowed dreams come from four places:

### From your parents

"You should be a doctor." "When are you getting married?" "When are you having kids?"

Their dreams for your life were shaped by their fears, their era, their constraints. They're not wrong. They're just not yours.

Their input is data. Not instruction.

### From your culture

"A successful woman has a career AND a family AND looks amazing AND gives back AND maintains a perfect home AND is always available AND never complains."

That's not a target state.

That's a trap dressed up as ambition.

No human being can optimize all of those simultaneously. The right decision means choosing which ones matter MOST to YOU. Which means some of them drop to "good enough" or "not right now."

That's not failure. That's honesty.

### From Instagram

You see someone's Tuesday and you want it.

But you're seeing a frame. Not a life.

You don't see their constraints. The tradeoffs they made. The things they gave up. Whether they're actually happy or just good at photos.

### From your partner

"We should move to the suburbs." "We should save for a house." "We should have kids by 35."

Check: do YOU want this?

Or have you absorbed their timeline as yours?

A decision made from your partner's target, not your own, is not a decision. It's compliance. And compliance doesn't produce commitment.

## Four questions that filter borrowed targets

For each condition in your target state, run it through these:

**1. If nobody would ever know, would I still want this?**

If the answer is no, you want it for status, not for you.

**2. Where did this first come from? Can I trace it to a specific person or moment?**

If you can trace it to your mother's voice, your culture's expectations, or a specific Instagram post — examine whether you chose it or inherited it.

**3. Am I pursuing this because I want it, or because I'm afraid of what people would say if I didn't?**

Fear of judgment is not the same as desire. They feel similar. They're not.

**4. Would I still want this if I lived on a desert island?**

Extreme question. But clarifying. Status goals vanish on the desert island. Real desires remain.

## You can love your life and still want a different one

I want to say this clearly because the self-help world gets it wrong:

**Being grateful and wanting more are not opposites.**

You can appreciate where you are AND acknowledge that something needs to change.

Gratitude is not resignation. Contentment is not giving up. "I love my life" and "I want something different" can live in the same sentence.

The problem is when you use one to avoid the other.

"I'm grateful for what I have" becomes code for "I've stopped trying."

"I want more" becomes code for "I'm never satisfied."

Both are true. Hold both.

The target state you defined in the Tuesday Test — is it yours? Is it real? Or is it a version of someone else's Tuesday that you absorbed without questioning?

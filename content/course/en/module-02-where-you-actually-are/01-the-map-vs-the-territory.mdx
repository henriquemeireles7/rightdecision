# The Map vs The Territory

## Maria's first version vs the truth

Maria is 36. Marketing manager at a startup. She came to us because she "felt stuck."

I asked her to write down where she was in life. Five minutes. Be honest.

Here's what she wrote:

> "I'm doing well at work but feeling unfulfilled. My relationship is stable. I'm healthy but could exercise more. Financially I'm okay."

Four sentences. All stories. Zero facts.

Then I asked her to try again. This time with numbers. Not narratives. Facts.

Here's version two:

> **Career:** Marketing manager, 3 years, same company. Passed over for promotion twice. My manager takes credit for my campaigns. Salary: $4,200/month. I update LinkedIn every few months but never actually apply. Rating: 3/10.
>
> **Relationship:** 2-year partnership. We eat dinner together but talk about logistics, not feelings. I haven't told him I'm unhappy because I don't want to deal with the conversation. Rating: 5/10.
>
> **Health:** 15 lbs heavier than last year. Insomnia 5 nights/week. Doctor says nothing is physically wrong. Rating: 4/10.
>
> **Finances:** $4,200 salary. $800 in savings. $12K student debt at 6.8%. No retirement savings. I spend $200/month on courses and self-help books. Rating: 3/10.
>
> **Emotional:** I feel like I'm running but going nowhere. Sunday nights I dread Monday. I cry in the shower sometimes but don't tell anyone. Rating: 3/10.

Same person. Same life. Completely different map.

The first version protects her. The second version informs her decisions.

**Every decision you make is only as good as the map you're making it from.** If the map is a lie, everything built on it is built on a lie.

## Why you lie to yourself (and how to stop)

You're not lying on purpose. Three things are happening in your brain:

### 1. The audience in your head

Even when nobody will read it, you write for an imaginary audience. You write what you'd want someone to see. "Doing well at work." "Relationship is stable."

**The fix:** Before each answer, say out loud: "Nobody will ever read this." Then write what you'd say to your best friend at 2am after two glasses of wine.

### 2. Admitting it means acting on it

If you write "$800 in savings and $12K in debt," you can't unknow that. You'll have to do something about it.

Your brain protects you from that pressure by softening the facts. "Financially I'm okay" is your brain's way of saying "please don't make me deal with this today."

**The fix:** Write the number anyway. Knowing it doesn't mean you have to fix it today. But you can't fix what you can't see.

### 3. The fox and the grapes

You stop wanting what you believe you can't have.

"I used to want a career change, but honestly, I'm grateful for what I have."

Did you really stop wanting it? Or did you give up and call it gratitude?

Both things can be true at the same time. You can be grateful for what you have AND want something different. Gratitude is not the same as resignation.

**The fix:** Ask yourself: "Is this contentment, or did I just stop trying?"

## What an honest map looks like

An honest state map has five things:

1. **Facts, not stories.** Numbers, dates, specifics.
2. **Uncomfortable truths.** The parts you don't tell people at dinner.
3. **Ratings.** A simple 1-10 for each area of your life.
4. **No softening.** Not "could exercise more." Instead: "gained 15 lbs, sleep 5 hours, no exercise."
5. **The thing you're pretending is fine.** Every map has one. Name it.

Maria's honest map hurt to read. That's how she knew it was true.

If your map doesn't make you slightly uncomfortable, it's probably not honest enough.

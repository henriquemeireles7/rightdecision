# One or Two Habits, Not Ten

## Maria installed one habit. It carried her through eight weeks.

"After my morning coffee, I spend 30 minutes on job search activities."

That was it. One habit. Every morning. Triggered by something she was already doing.

Some mornings she researched companies. Some mornings she tweaked her resume. Some mornings she practiced interview answers. The specific activity changed. The 30-minute block after coffee never did.

She didn't install five new habits. She didn't overhaul her morning routine. She didn't download a habit tracker app with 47 daily checkboxes.

One habit. Attached to coffee.

When her motivation dipped (and it did, around week 3), the habit didn't need motivation. It needed coffee. And she was going to have coffee anyway.

## Why habits matter for decisions

Here's the problem with decisions alone.

You make the decision with energy on a Monday. By the following Monday, the energy is gone. Not because you're weak. Because that's how motivation works. It spikes and fades. Spikes and fades.

If your plan depends entirely on motivation, it will work for about 10 days and then collapse.

Habits don't need motivation. They need a trigger.

- Motivation says: "I feel inspired to work on my career today!"
- A habit says: "It's after coffee. I sit down for 30 minutes. That's what I do now."

**Decisions set the direction. Habits keep you walking when you forget why you started.**

Without the decision, the habit has no purpose. You're walking but going nowhere.

Without the habit, the decision evaporates. You know where to go but you're not moving.

You need both.

## The habit formula

**"After I [thing I already do], I will [new thing] for [specific duration]."**

Three parts. All essential.

### The trigger (most important part)

Not "every morning." Not "when I feel like it." Not "three times a week."

**After a specific existing behavior.**

- After I pour my coffee
- After I close my laptop at 6pm
- After I park at the gym
- After I brush my teeth at night
- After I sit down on the train

The trigger must be something you do EVERY DAY without thinking. You're hijacking an existing neural pathway. The new behavior gets a free ride on the old one.

### The behavior (keep it small)

Not "work on my business plan." That's too vague and too big.

"Write one paragraph of my business plan." That's 5 minutes. Tiny. Undeniable.

The behavior should be so small that you feel slightly ridiculous about it. That's the right size.

Why? Because on the bad days — the exhausted days, the stressed days, the "I don't feel like it" days — you'll still do it. One paragraph. Five minutes. You can do that half-asleep.

And once you start, you usually keep going. But even if you don't, you did the minimum. The chain stays unbroken.

### The duration (set the floor, not the ceiling)

"At least 15 minutes." Not "one hour."

The floor is the minimum you do no matter what. The ceiling is wherever your energy takes you.

Maria's floor was 30 minutes after coffee. Some days she did 30 minutes. Some days she got into a flow and did 90. The 30-minute floor meant she never did zero.

## Why not five habits?

Because the research is clear: humans can install 1-2 new habits at a time.

Try to install five simultaneously and here's what happens:

- Week 1: you do all five. You feel amazing. You tell your friends.
- Week 2: you miss one. Then another. You feel guilty.
- Week 3: you're doing two out of five. The guilt makes you stop all of them.
- Week 4: zero habits remain. You're back where you started. Plus shame.

**One or two habits. That's the limit.** Not because you're weak. Because that's how human behavior works. The research on this is consistent across decades.

Pick the ONE habit that supports your decision most directly.

If your decision is about career: a daily job search habit.

If your decision is about finances: a daily expense-tracking habit.

If your decision is about health: a daily movement habit.

One. Attached to coffee. Or to parking. Or to brushing teeth.

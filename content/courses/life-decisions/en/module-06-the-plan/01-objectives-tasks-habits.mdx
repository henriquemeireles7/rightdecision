# Objectives, Tasks, Habits

## Maria's decision was "change my career." Her plan fit on one page.

After Maria identified her constraint (career, 3/10) and made her decision ("Apply to three marketing director positions by April 30th"), she needed a plan.

Not a 15-page strategy document. Not a vision board. Not a color-coded Notion setup.

A plan that answers one question: **what do I do tomorrow morning?**

Here's what she wrote on a single sheet of paper:

**Objectives:**
- Get 3 applications submitted (metric: 3 confirmation emails)
- Prepare for interviews (metric: 2 mock interviews done)
- Negotiate salary above $6,000/month (metric: offer letter)

**Tasks (in order):**
1. List 10 target companies — *Tuesday evening*
2. Update resume — *Wednesday evening*
3. Send resume to Julia for feedback — *Thursday morning*
4. Revise resume based on feedback — *Saturday afternoon*
5. Apply to company #1 — *Sunday*
6. Apply to companies #2 and #3 — *Monday and Tuesday*
7. Schedule mock interview with Julia — *by Wednesday*
8. Second mock interview with Ricardo — *by Friday*

**Habits:**
- After morning coffee, spend 30 minutes on job search activities

One page. That's the whole plan.

## Three layers, three different jobs

Every plan has three layers. They look similar but do completely different things.

### Objectives tell you WHAT success looks like

An objective is a measurable outcome. It has a number attached to it.

- "Get 3 applications submitted" — you can count them
- "Save $2,000 in 3 months" — you can check the bank account
- "Have the conversation with mom" — it happened or it didn't

Not: "Feel better about my career." How would you measure that?

Not: "Work on my finances." Work on them how? When? What's the number?

**If you can't put a number on it, it's not an objective. It's a wish wearing professional clothing.**

### Tasks tell you WHAT TO DO today

A task is a specific action with a deadline that you can finish in one sitting.

The test: **Could a camera record you doing it?**

- "Update my resume" — yes, a camera could record you typing
- "Work on my career" — what does that look like on camera? Nothing.
- "Research options" — vague. Research what? Where? For how long?

Each task must be:

1. **Specific.** Starts with a verb. "Call," "write," "send," "schedule."
2. **Time-bound.** Has a day. Not "this week." A day.
3. **Completable in under 2 hours.** If it takes longer, break it down further.

If your task is "figure out my life," that's not a task. That's an existential crisis pretending to be a to-do item.

### Habits tell you HOW TO SUSTAIN it

Decisions without habits decay. You make the decision with energy and conviction on a Monday. By Thursday the energy is gone. By the following Monday you've forgotten why it mattered.

A habit keeps the system running when motivation disappears.

The formula: **"After I [existing behavior], I will [new behavior]."**

- "After my morning coffee, I spend 30 minutes on job search."
- "After I close my laptop at 6pm, I write one paragraph of my business plan."
- "After I park at the gym, I text Julia what I'm about to do."

The trigger is more important than the habit. You already drink coffee. You already close your laptop. You already park at the gym. The new behavior rides on the existing one.

**One or two habits at a time. Not five. Not ten.** Research says humans can install one, maybe two new habits simultaneously. Any more and they all collapse.

*What's one existing behavior you could attach your first task to?*

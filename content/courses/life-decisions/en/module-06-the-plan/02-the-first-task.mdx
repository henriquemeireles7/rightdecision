# The First Task

## The most important question in any plan

Here it is:

**"What do I do tomorrow morning?"**

Not this month. Not this week. Tomorrow. At what time. For how long. Specifically.

If your plan can't answer that question, it's not a plan. It's a document.

Maria's plan answered it immediately: "Tomorrow evening, I list 10 target companies. 30 minutes. At my kitchen table after dinner."

That's it. That's the whole first task.

And that first task is the most important one in the entire plan. Not because it's the hardest. Because it's the one that turns "I decided" into "I'm doing."

## Why the first task matters more than the last one

There's a gap between deciding and doing. It's usually about 24 hours wide.

You make the decision on Monday with energy and clarity. By Tuesday morning, the voice starts:

"Maybe I should research more first."

"Maybe I should wait until after the holidays."

"Maybe I should do one more module before I start."

These are all Sin #3. Disguising indecision as preparation.

The first task is the bridge across that gap. If you know EXACTLY what to do tomorrow, the voice has nothing to work with. It can't say "but what would you even do?" because you already know.

**Clarity kills hesitation.**

Maria didn't have to think about what to do on Tuesday evening. She already knew: list 10 companies. 30 minutes. Kitchen table. After dinner.

No decision fatigue. No ambiguity. Just execution.

## What makes a good first task

Three qualities:

**1. Small enough to be undeniable.**

Not "redo my entire resume." That's overwhelming. That produces procrastination.

"List 10 target companies." That's 30 minutes. Anyone can do it. There's no excuse not to.

The first task should be so small that NOT doing it would be embarrassing. Like... you literally have 30 minutes. Come on.

**2. Clearly the beginning.**

It should be obvious that this is step 1 and everything else follows from it. Maria can't apply to companies if she hasn't listed them. The first task creates the domino.

**3. Completable tonight or tomorrow.**

Not "this weekend." Not "when I have time." Tonight. Or tomorrow morning.

The gap between deciding and doing must be as short as possible. Every hour of delay is an hour for the voice to build a case against you.

## The two sins that kill plans

**Sin #4: Deciding without decomposing.**

"I'm going to change my life!"

Great. What are you doing at 9am tomorrow? *Silence.*

This is the motivational high. The bold declaration at 11pm on a Sunday. The Instagram post about your "new chapter." Three days later: nothing happened. The energy dissipated. The declaration had no structure underneath it.

**A decision without a plan is a firework. Beautiful for a moment. Gone by morning.**

**Sin #5: Decomposing without deciding.**

Beautiful spreadsheet. Color-coded task list. Categorized by priority. Dependencies mapped.

But you never actually committed. The plan is there. The decision isn't.

This is productive theater. You feel busy. You feel organized. But you never said "I WILL do this by FRIDAY" and told someone about it.

**A plan without a decision is a script for a play nobody's performing.**

You need both. The decision from Module 5 AND the plan from this module.

*Do you have both? The commitment AND the first task?*

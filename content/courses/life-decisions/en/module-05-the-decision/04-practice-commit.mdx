# Practice: Commit

## The fifth document. The center of everything.

This is the one the entire course has been building toward.

Your Discomfort Map told you something is wrong. Your State Map told you where you actually are. Your Target State told you where you want to be. Your Constraint Identification told you what's in the way.

Now you decide what to do about it.

## The questions

Eight questions. Take your time. But set a deadline: this is done today. Not tomorrow. Not "when I'm ready."

Today.

### Part 1: The decision (Questions 1-3)

**Q1. Look at your dominant constraint. What is the decision that resolves it?**

Write it as: "I will [specific action] by [date]."

A bad answer: "I will work on my career." (No action. No date. Not a decision.)

A good answer: "I will apply to three marketing director positions by April 30th."

An even better answer: "I will update my resume this Saturday, send it to Julia for feedback on Sunday, and apply to three companies by next Friday."

**Q2. Is this specific enough that someone else could verify whether you did it?**

If a stranger looked at your life on the deadline date, could they clearly see whether this happened or not?

"Work on my career" — unverifiable. How would a stranger know?

"Apply to three companies" — verifiable. Either there are three application confirmation emails or there aren't.

**Q3. Set the date. Write it on your calendar. Right now.**

Not "soon." Not "this month." A specific day.

If you just thought "I'll set the date later" — that's Sin #3. You're delaying the commitment with a micro-delay. Set it now.

### Part 2: The risk (Questions 4-5)

**Q4. What is the realistic worst case if this decision goes wrong?**

Not the catastrophe version. The realistic one.

A bad answer: "Everything falls apart and I end up homeless." (Catastrophizing. Not realistic.)

A good answer: "I apply to three jobs and get rejected by all three. I'm still in my current job, same as before. I lose nothing except the time spent applying."

**Q5. What is the realistic worst case if you NEVER make this decision?**

A bad answer: "Things stay the same." (That's not the worst case. That's the best case of not deciding.)

A good answer: "In two years I'm still in this job, still not sleeping, still dreading Monday, still resentful with Carlos. I'm two years older with $0 more saved and the job market is harder. The cost of this decision is $67,600 in lost income over 26 months — and counting."

Now compare. Which worst case is actually worse?

### Part 3: The witness (Questions 6-7)

**Q6. Who will you tell?**

One person. Name them.

Not your journal. Not social media. A human being who will check in.

Pick someone who won't let you off the hook. Not the friend who says "aww, it's okay, take your time." The friend who'll text you on the deadline date and say "did you do it?"

A bad answer: "I'll tell people when I'm further along."

A good answer: "I'll tell Julia. She'll ask me about it."

**Q7. When will you tell them?**

Today. Tomorrow at the latest.

Write down: "I will tell [name] about my decision on [day]."

### Part 4: The final check (Question 8)

**Q8. Say your full decision statement out loud right now. "I will ___ by ___. I'm telling ___ about it on ___."**

What happened in your body?

A bad sign: nothing. You felt nothing. It might not be the real decision.

A good sign: your chest tightened. Your voice caught. You felt scared. Or excited. Or both.

**That feeling is the confirmation that it's real.**

## After answering: the AI step

The AI's job here is to be your honest friend. The one who says "is that really specific enough?" and "are you softening it?"

### Copy this prompt:

```
You are a decision commitment coach for The Right Decision methodology. I just wrote my decision statement — the specific commitment I'm making to resolve my dominant constraint.

Your job is to make sure this decision is REAL — specific enough to execute, scary enough to matter, and witnessed enough to stick.

Do four things:

1. CHECK THE DECISION STATEMENT. Does it have:
   - A specific action? (Not "work on" or "try to." A verb a camera could record.)
   - A date? (Not "soon." A calendar day.)
   - Enough specificity that a stranger could verify it happened?
   If anything is missing or vague, rewrite it for me as an example and ask: "Is this more accurate?"

2. CHECK THE RISK ASSESSMENT. Compare my two worst cases:
   - Worst case if I decide wrong
   - Worst case if I never decide
   If the "never decide" case is clearly worse (it usually is), point that out explicitly. If I'm catastrophizing the "decide wrong" case, call it out.

3. CHECK THE WITNESS. Did I name a real person? Will that person actually follow up? If I said "I'll tell people later" — push back. "Later" means never.

4. PRODUCE MY DECISION DOCUMENT:

   ## DECISION STATEMENT — [Date]

   **My decision:** "I will [action] by [date]."
   **My witness:** [name], telling them on [date]
   **Realistic downside of deciding:** [from my answer]
   **Realistic downside of NOT deciding:** [from my answer]
   **Risk assessment:** [asymmetric — survivable downside, transformational upside? Or symmetric?]
   **Body check:** [what I felt when I said it out loud]
   **Commitment level (1-10):** [my self-assessment]

   If my commitment level is below 7, ask me: "What would need to change for this to be a 9?"

Be direct. If I'm hedging, say so. If I'm ready, confirm it.

Here are my answers:
```

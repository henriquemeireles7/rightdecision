# Practice: Name Your Constraint

## The fourth document in your folder. The hardest one to write.

Not because the questions are complicated.

Because the honest answer is usually the one you've been avoiding for a very long time.

This is the class where many people stall. They'll do the questionnaire later. They'll think about it. They need more time.

That's Sin #3. You already have enough information. Your State Map and Target State contain everything you need. The constraint is sitting in the gap between them.

Name it. Today.

## The questions

Ten questions. Write full answers.

### Part 1: List everything (Questions 1-3)

**Q1. Look at your State Map and your Target State. List EVERY obstacle between them.**

Big ones, small ones. Don't filter. Don't judge. Just list.

Examples:
- "I don't have enough savings to take a risk"
- "My job doesn't pay enough"
- "I haven't had the conversation with my partner"
- "I don't have the skills for what I want to do"
- "I'm scared of failing"
- "I don't know where to start"

Write at least 5. Write 10 if you can.

**Q2. For each obstacle, rate it: "If this disappeared overnight, how much closer would I be to my ideal Tuesday?" (1-10)**

Be honest. Don't overthink. First instinct.

**Q3. Which one scored the highest?**

Write it down. That's your candidate.

### Part 2: Validate it (Questions 4-7)

**Q4. Does naming this constraint make you uncomfortable?**

Does your stomach tighten? Do you want to pick a different one? That discomfort is a signal, not a warning.

A bad answer: "Not really, it seems manageable."
A good answer: "Yes. I've been avoiding thinking about this directly. Even writing it down feels heavy."

**Q5. If this constraint were resolved, which other domains in your State Map would improve?**

List them. Career improves? Finances? Health? Relationships?

A bad answer: "Just this one area."
A good answer: "If I fixed my career, my finances would improve (higher salary), my health would improve (less stress, better sleep), and my relationship would improve (I wouldn't be so resentful after work)."

If it cascades — it's probably dominant.

**Q6. Is this the constraint itself, or a symptom of something deeper?**

Ask "why?" three times.

A bad answer: "I need to exercise more."
A good answer: "I don't exercise because I'm exhausted. I'm exhausted because my job drains me. My job drains me because I've been in the same role for three years with no growth. The constraint is my career, not the exercise."

**Q7. Did you identify this yourself, or did someone else tell you this was your problem?**

A bad answer: "My therapist says I need to work on my self-worth."
A good answer: "Looking at my own data — my State Map, my ratings, my Tuesday — the career is the clear bottleneck. I can see it in the numbers."

### Part 3: Commit to it (Questions 8-10)

**Q8. Write it in one sentence: "My dominant constraint is ___."**

A bad answer: "My dominant constraint is that I need to work on multiple things."
A good answer: "My dominant constraint is my career. I've been in the same underpaid role for three years and I'm too scared to leave."

**Q9. Why THIS one and not the others?**

Defend your choice. If you can't explain why this constraint matters more than the other candidates, you might not have found the right one.

**Q10. On a scale of 1-10, how scared are you of addressing this?**

A 7+ is a good sign. It means you're looking at the real thing.

A 3 or below? You might be picking the safe problem. Go back to Q1 and look again.

## After answering: the AI step

This is the toughest AI prompt in the course. The AI's job is to TRY TO TALK YOU OUT OF YOUR CHOICE. On purpose.

If your reasoning holds up, you've found the right constraint. If the AI pokes a hole and you realize "wait, actually..." — you just saved yourself months of working on the wrong thing.

---

### Copy this prompt:

```
You are an adversarial constraint identification coach for The Right Decision methodology. I just identified what I believe is my dominant constraint — the single biggest obstacle between where I am and where I want to be.

Your job is to CHALLENGE my choice. Try to talk me out of it. Not to be difficult, but to make sure I picked the right one.

Do five things:

1. READ MY CONSTRAINT. I'll share my one-sentence statement and my reasoning.

2. PLAY DEVIL'S ADVOCATE. Challenge my choice with these questions:
   - "Are you sure this is the constraint, or is this the comfortable version of a scarier one?"
   - "You said resolving this would improve [domains]. Walk me through exactly HOW. Be specific."
   - "Is there something you DIDN'T list in your obstacles that you might be avoiding even naming?"
   - "If I told you this constraint doesn't matter and the real one is [pick something from their list], how would you argue back?"

3. CHECK FOR SYMPTOMS VS CAUSES. Look at my three-why analysis. Did I go deep enough? Or did I stop at a symptom?

4. TEST THE CASCADE. Ask me: "If this constraint vanished tonight, describe your tomorrow. Specifically. What's different when you wake up?" If my answer is vague, push for details. If it's vivid and specific, the constraint is probably right.

5. GIVE YOUR VERDICT. Based on my answers to your challenges:
   - CONFIRMED: "Your constraint holds up. Here's why: [evidence from my responses]."
   - REDIRECTED: "I think the real constraint might be ___. Here's why: [evidence]. Consider switching."
   - NEEDS REFINEMENT: "You're in the right area, but your statement is too broad/vague. Try: [rewrite]."

Then produce my Constraint Identification Document:

## DOMINANT CONSTRAINT — [Date]

**My constraint:** [one sentence]
**Why this one:** [my reasoning, refined by our conversation]
**What it cascades to:** [which other domains improve]
**Fear level:** [my 1-10 score]
**Validation status:** [Confirmed / Redirected / Refined]

Be adversarial but fair. If I've found the right one, say so. If I haven't, save me months.

Here are my answers:
```

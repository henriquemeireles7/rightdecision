# The Misidentification Trap

## The most expensive mistake in this course

Picking the wrong constraint.

Not because you can't recover. You can. But a full cycle wasted on the wrong problem costs months. You'll go through decomposition, execution, even get results... and at the end realize that you solved something real, but it wasn't the thing that was actually holding you back.

Your life feels the same. Your Tuesday looks the same. The discomfort didn't budge.

That's the misidentification trap. And it happens more often than you'd think.

## How it happens

Three common ways:

### 1. You pick the comfortable constraint

This is Sin #1 again. The real constraint scares you, so you pick the adjacent one.

"My problem is that I need better boundaries at work."

Maybe. Or maybe your problem is that you're at the wrong job entirely, and better boundaries won't change that.

Boundaries is the comfortable version. Career change is the scary version.

**The test:** If you resolved this constraint perfectly, would your Tuesday description from Module 3 become real? If yes, it's the right one. If you'd still be far from your Tuesday... it's probably the comfortable version.

### 2. You pick a symptom, not the cause

"My problem is that I don't exercise enough."

Is it? Or is the lack of exercise a symptom of exhaustion, which is a symptom of a career that drains you, which is the actual constraint?

Symptoms are downstream. The constraint is upstream.

**The test:** Ask "why?" three times.
- Why don't I exercise? *Because I'm tired after work.*
- Why am I tired after work? *Because my job is draining and I dread it.*
- Why is my job draining? *Because I've been doing the same thing for three years with no growth.*

The third "why" usually gets you to the constraint. The first answer is almost always a symptom.

### 3. You pick someone else's constraint

"My problem is that I need to heal my inner child."

Says who? Your therapist? A book? An Instagram post?

Sometimes "heal the inner child" IS the constraint. But often it's someone else's framework applied to your situation. Their diagnostic lens, not your reality.

**The test:** Did you identify this constraint yourself, from your state map and target state? Or did someone else tell you this was your problem?

If someone told you — go back to your data. What does YOUR map say?

## How Maria almost got it wrong

Remember: Maria first said her constraint was the relationship.

If she had spent three months working on the relationship with Carlos — couples therapy, communication workshops, date nights — she would have made some progress. The relationship might have gone from 5/10 to 6/10.

But her career would still be a 3. Her finances still a 3. Her insomnia still there. Her Sunday dread untouched.

She'd have "worked on herself" for three months and still felt stuck.

The relationship was a real problem. It just wasn't the dominant constraint.

**The dominant constraint is not necessarily the biggest problem. It's the problem whose resolution moves everything else.**

## Validating your choice

Before you commit to a constraint in the practice class, run these three checks:

**Check 1: The Tuesday test.**

If this constraint disappeared overnight, how much closer would you be to your ideal Tuesday? If the answer is less than 7 out of 10... it might not be the dominant one.

**Check 2: The gut check.**

Does naming this constraint make you uncomfortable? Slightly scared? Do you want to look at a different one instead?

Good. That discomfort is a signal. The real constraint is usually the one you want to avoid looking at.

**Check 3: The cascade check.**

Would resolving this constraint improve at least one other domain in your state map? If yes, it's likely dominant. If resolving it changes nothing else... it might be a symptom.

Maria's constraint (career) passed all three:
- Tuesday test: 9/10 closer
- Gut check: stomach drop, wanted to talk about Carlos instead
- Cascade: career fix improved finances, health, emotional state, and even the relationship

Next class: you're going to name yours. Write it down. Make it real. The AI will try to talk you out of it — on purpose.

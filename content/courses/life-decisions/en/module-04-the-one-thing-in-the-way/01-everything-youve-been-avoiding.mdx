# Everything You've Been Avoiding

## Maria thought her problem was her relationship. It wasn't.

Remember Maria? She scored 18 out of 50 on her state map. Career: 3. Relationship: 5. Health: 4. Finances: 3. Emotional: 3.

When I asked her "What's the biggest thing standing between where you are and where you want to be?" she said immediately:

"My relationship. If I could fix things with Carlos, everything else would fall into place."

She was wrong.

Not because her relationship was fine. It wasn't. But when we looked at her state map closely, something was off.

Her career was a 3. She'd been passed over for promotion twice. Her manager took credit for her work. She hadn't updated her resume in two years. She dreaded Monday mornings so badly it ruined her Sunday nights.

Her relationship was a 5. Not great. But stable. Functional.

So why was she pointing at the 5 instead of the 3?

**Because the 3 was scarier.**

Fixing the career meant job searching. Interviews. Rejection. Telling her manager she was leaving. The unknown.

Fixing the relationship meant... talking to Carlos. Uncomfortable, yes. But familiar. Safe.

She was solving the easier problem because the real one terrified her.

## The safe problem trap

This is Sin #1, and it's the most common pattern I see.

You have multiple problems. Everyone does. But there's usually ONE that, if resolved, would make everything else smaller. We call it the dominant constraint.

And it's almost always the one you're avoiding.

How do you know you're avoiding it? Three tells:

1. **You rated it fast.** In Module 2, the domain you scored in under 5 seconds is the one you didn't want to look at.

2. **You're overinvesting in something else.** Perfect morning routine. Crushing it at the gym. Reading 30 books a year. All while the real problem sits untouched.

3. **Your body knows.** Remember Module 1? The insomnia, the anxiety, the Sunday dread? Those signals usually point at the dominant constraint, not the problem you're actively working on.

Maria's insomnia wasn't about Carlos. It was about the job. Her body had been telling her for months. She just wasn't listening because "fix the relationship" felt safer than "change my career."

## What is a dominant constraint?

It's the single biggest obstacle between your current state and your target state.

Not the loudest problem. Not the most urgent one. Not the one your friends keep asking about.

**The one that, if magically resolved overnight, would create the most forward movement in your life.**

Think about that.

If your career constraint disappeared tomorrow, would your relationship improve? Would your health improve? Would your energy come back?

For Maria, the answer was yes to all three. The career was dragging everything else down. The relationship wasn't great, but it wasn't the bottleneck.

*What about you? Which of your five domains, if fixed, would make the others easier?*

## How to find yours

You already have the data. It's in your State Map and your Target State from Act I.

Here's the exercise:

**Step 1.** List every obstacle between where you are and where you want to be. Big, small, all of them. Write them all down. Don't filter.

**Step 2.** For each one, ask: "If this disappeared overnight, how much closer would I be to my Tuesday?" Rate it 1-10.

**Step 3.** Look at the one with the highest score.

**Step 4.** The gut check. Does looking at that item make your stomach tighten? Does it make you want to look at a different item?

If yes — that's probably it.

If you feel nothing — look again. The real constraint is usually the one that produces a physical reaction. Not excitement. Not dread. Something. A tightness. An "I don't want to think about that."

Maria's gut check: career scored 9. Relationship scored 5. Career made her stomach drop. She wanted to talk about Carlos instead.

The stomach drop was the answer.

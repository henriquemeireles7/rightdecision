# Wake Up, Do the Thing, Go to Sleep

## The entire execution protocol is three steps

That's it. Three steps. Every day.

1. Check: what's today's task?
2. Do it.
3. Record: did I do it? Yes or no.

Wake up. Do the thing. Go to sleep.

No journaling about how you feel about the task. No analyzing whether the task is the right task. No planning a better way to do the task. Just do the task.

This is the simplest module in the course.

And the most important.

## Maria's first week

Monday. First task: list 10 target companies. She sat at the kitchen table after dinner. Opened her laptop. Wrote 10 company names in 25 minutes.

Done.

Tuesday. Second task: update resume summary section. 40 minutes.

Done.

Wednesday. Third task: send resume to Julia for feedback. One email. 3 minutes.

Done.

Thursday. Nothing on the plan until Saturday. She kept her habit — 30 minutes after coffee — and used it to research the 10 companies.

Friday. Same.

Saturday. Fourth task: revise resume based on Julia's feedback. 50 minutes.

Done.

That's it. That was her first week. No breakthroughs. No dramatic moments. No music playing. Just a woman doing one thing each day at her kitchen table.

**And yet — more happened in that week than in 26 months of thinking about it.**

## Why one task, not five

You might be thinking: "I could do more. I have time. Let me knock out three tasks today."

Don't.

Here's why.

If you do three tasks today, tomorrow you'll feel like you should do three again. And the day after. You've set the bar at three.

Then a bad day comes. Stress, fatigue, life. You do zero.

Now you're behind. Three-per-day becomes guilt becomes avoidance becomes "I'll restart Monday" becomes another month of nothing.

**One task per day is the pace that survives bad days.**

On your best day: you do the task and feel great.

On your worst day: you do the task and move on.

Same result. One step forward. Every single day.

The streak is not the point. The direction is the point.

## The reward is the doing

I need to tell you something that goes against everything the self-help industry teaches.

**The reward is not the result. The reward is the execution itself.**

Not "I'll feel amazing when I get the job offer."

"I feel something right now because I'm doing the thing."

Indy says it better than I do: "It's a feeling of relief that you are closer to what you want. Like I am already better than yesterday."

That feeling — "I am already better than yesterday" — that's the reward. You don't have to wait for the outcome. The doing IS the outcome. Each day you do the thing, you are literally a different person than the person who didn't do it.

*When was the last time you went to bed knowing you did the one thing that mattered most? How did that feel?*

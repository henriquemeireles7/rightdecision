# When You Miss a Day

## Nothing happens. That's the whole class.

You missed a day.

You didn't do the task. Maybe you were sick. Maybe the day was brutal. Maybe you just didn't feel like it.

Here's what you do:

**Tomorrow's task. Tomorrow.**

That's it.

You don't start over. You don't redo the plan. You don't beat yourself up. You don't journal about why you failed. You don't owe anyone an explanation.

You just do tomorrow's task tomorrow.

## Why this matters more than it sounds

Most self-development programs treat a missed day like a catastrophe.

"You broke the chain!" "You lost your streak!" "Start from Day 1 again!"

This produces a pattern I've seen destroy more plans than any external obstacle:

1. Miss one day
2. Feel guilty
3. Miss another day because the guilt makes it feel pointless
4. "I'll restart Monday"
5. Monday comes. More guilt. "I'll restart next month."
6. Three months later: "That didn't work for me."

**The program didn't fail. The guilt loop killed it.**

The Right Decision has no streaks. No chains. No Day 1 restarts.

You have a direction. Some days you take a step. Some days you don't. The direction doesn't change because you missed a day.

## Minimum viable progress

On the days when everything is hard — sick kid, bad news, exhaustion that sleep won't fix — you don't need to do the full task.

You need the minimum viable version.

Maria's daily habit was 30 minutes of job search after coffee. On her worst day (a migraine), her minimum viable version was:

"Open the job search tab. Look at one listing."

Two minutes. Didn't even apply. Just looked.

Did that move the needle? Barely.

Did it keep the loop alive? Yes.

**Because the hardest part of doing the thing is never the thing itself. It's starting.** Two minutes of starting is infinitely more than zero minutes of planning to start tomorrow.

What's your minimum viable version?

Take your daily habit from Module 6. Now shrink it to its smallest possible form:

- 30 minutes of job search → "Open the tab and look at one listing"
- 30 minutes of writing → "Write one sentence"
- 30 minutes of exercise → "Put on the shoes and walk to the end of the block"

That's your floor. On the worst day, you do the floor. On every other day, you do the real thing.

**The floor is not the goal. The floor is the insurance policy against quitting.**

## The all-or-nothing trap

"I either do it perfectly or I don't do it at all."

Recognize that?

This sounds like discipline. It's actually fear.

All-or-nothing thinking means that any imperfect day feels like failure. And failure feels like permission to stop.

"I ate one cookie so the diet is ruined. I'll start again Monday." Meanwhile you eat the whole box.

"I missed my workout so the week is ruined. I'll start again next week." Meanwhile you don't move for 10 days.

**The antidote to all-or-nothing is minimum viable progress.** A bad workout is better than no workout. One sentence is better than no writing. Looking at one listing is better than closing the laptop.

Progress is not binary. It's a spectrum. And anything above zero counts.

*Have you been treating your plan as all-or-nothing? What would change if you gave yourself permission to do the minimum on bad days instead of doing nothing?*

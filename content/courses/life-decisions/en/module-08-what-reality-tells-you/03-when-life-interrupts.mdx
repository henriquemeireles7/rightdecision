# When Life Interrupts

## Your old life isn't ready for the new you

You're in Week 3. Executing. Making progress. Things are moving.

Then something happens.

Your car breaks down. Your kid gets sick. Your partner starts a fight about "how different you've been lately." You get an unexpected bill. Your mother calls with a crisis.

The disruption feels massive. It fills your whole screen. Every other priority disappears.

The instinct is to pause the loop. "I need to deal with this first. I'll come back to the plan when things stabilize."

**Don't.**

Not because the disruption doesn't matter. It does. But pausing the loop is one of the most dangerous moves you can make.

## Why pausing kills the loop

I've watched this pattern in myself and in every friend I've coached.

When someone pauses "to deal with life," here's what happens:

- **Week 1 of the pause:** They handle the crisis. Makes sense.
- **Week 2:** The crisis is mostly handled. But restarting the loop feels hard. "I'll start again Monday."
- **Week 3:** Monday came and went. The plan feels distant. The momentum is gone. "Maybe I need to redo the state mapping."
- **Month 2:** They haven't restarted. The old discomfort is back. They're stuck again. But now with extra guilt for having stopped.
- **Month 6:** They tell friends "I tried that decision thing, but life got in the way."

Life didn't get in the way. The pause did.

**The loop never pauses.** That's the rule. Not because you're a machine. Because pausing is how the loop dies.

## The disruption is not your new constraint

This is the mistake.

Something disruptive happens. It's loud, urgent, demanding. The instinct is to make it the new dominant constraint.

"My car broke down. That's my new constraint. I need to deal with this first."

Wait. Remember what a dominant constraint is from Module 4:

**The single biggest obstacle between your current state and your TARGET STATE.**

Did your target state change because your car broke down?

No. Your Tuesday description is the same. Your conditions are the same. Your life direction hasn't shifted.

The car is a disruption. Not a constraint. It's urgent. It needs handling. But it's not the thing standing between you and your Tuesday.

**When you make the disruption your new constraint, you're deciding from fear and urgency instead of from clarity and direction.**

The constraint is a byproduct of BOTH your starting point and your end point. If you ignore the end point and focus only on the urgent thing in front of you, every decision becomes reactive. You're playing whack-a-mole with life instead of building toward your Tuesday.

## What to do instead

**Step 1: Handle the disruption.** Obviously. Fix the car. Take care of the kid. Deal with the bill. This isn't optional.

**Step 2: Don't abandon the end state.** Keep your Target State visible. Even if you can't work on it this week. Knowing where you're going prevents you from getting lost in the detour.

**Step 3: Go back to state mapping.** Your initial state changed. The car breakdown or the unexpected bill shifted your reality. Update the map. Not the target, just the starting point.

**Step 4: Reassess whether the constraint changed.** Did the disruption actually change what's standing between you and your Tuesday?

Usually: no. The career constraint didn't disappear because your car broke down. It's still there, just temporarily overshadowed by the noise.

Sometimes: yes. If you lost your job, the constraint might genuinely shift from "career growth" to "financial survival." That's a real constraint change. Go back to Module 4 and reassess.

**Step 5: Resume at the minimum viable version.** Not the full 30 minutes. The 2-minute version. Keep the loop alive until the disruption passes.

## The "old life" resistance

Here's something nobody warns you about.

When you start changing, the people and systems around you resist. Not because they're evil. Because they're comfortable with the old version of you.

- Your partner gets uneasy because you're more focused and less available.
- Your friends feel weird because you're not complaining about the same things anymore.
- Your family worries because you're "acting different."

These disruptions LOOK like external problems. They're actually the friction that comes from outgrowing your current environment.

When the disruption is social friction from the people closest to you — that's not a sign to stop. It's proof that the decision is real.

Remember from Module 5: **if your decision doesn't rearrange your life, it wasn't a decision.**

The rearranging sometimes looks like friction with the people who were comfortable with the old arrangement.

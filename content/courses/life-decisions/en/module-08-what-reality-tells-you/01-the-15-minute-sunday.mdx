# The 15-Minute Sunday

## Maria's Week 2 review changed her entire plan

Maria had been executing for two weeks. Applying to jobs. Doing the 30-minute coffee habit. Checking tasks off.

On Sunday evening she sat down for 15 minutes and answered five questions.

By question 3, she realized something. Two of her three target companies were in an industry she didn't care about. She'd picked them because they were hiring, not because they matched her Tuesday description.

She was executing efficiently on the wrong targets.

Without the Sunday review, she would have kept applying. Maybe gotten an offer. Maybe accepted a job she didn't want. And ended up in the same place — stuck, but at a higher salary.

**Fifteen minutes saved her from a three-month detour.**

## Why weekly, not daily

Daily self-assessment becomes anxiety. You check in every night: "Am I making progress? Is this working? Am I doing enough?" Every day looks the same. Nothing moves fast enough to notice in 24 hours.

Weekly is the right cadence because:

- You have enough data. One day is noise. Seven days is a signal.
- You have enough distance. Sunday evening is far enough from Monday morning to see patterns.
- It's short enough to catch problems early. Monthly reviews let bad strategies run for too long.

**Every Sunday. Fifteen minutes. Five questions. That's the whole review.**

## The five questions

These are the same five questions every week. You'll memorize them by week 3.

**1. Look at your State Map from Module 2. What has changed?**

Not "how do you feel about your progress." What has ACTUALLY changed? Numbers. Facts.

Maria's Week 2: "Applied to 5 companies (was 0). Got 2 responses (was 0). Resume is updated (was outdated for 2 years). Sleep improved from 5/10 to 6/10."

Specific changes. Measurable.

**2. Are you closer to your Target State than last Sunday?**

Evidence, not feeling.

Maria: "Yes. Two companies responded. I'm closer to having a new job. But my target Tuesday describes work I believe in — and two of these companies don't match that."

This is where she caught the misalignment.

**3. Is the dominant constraint still the right one?**

Sometimes the constraint shifts. Life happens. A new obstacle emerges. The original constraint resolves partially and reveals a deeper one.

Maria: "Yes, career is still the bottleneck. But I need to narrow my targets."

If the answer is "no, the constraint changed" — you go back to Module 4. Not Module 1. You don't restart the whole loop. Just reassess the constraint.

**4. Are my daily tasks moving the needle?**

Not "am I doing the tasks." Are they producing results?

Maria: "Yes for the process (I'm applying). No for the direction (wrong companies). Need to change WHO I'm applying to, not HOW."

This distinction matters. Sometimes the system is working but pointed at the wrong target.

**5. Has anything major happened that changes my initial or end state?**

Job loss. Breakup. Health crisis. Unexpected opportunity.

Maria Week 2: "No major changes."

If the answer is "yes" — you might need to redo your state mapping. Not because you failed. Because the ground shifted.

## What the review produces

At the end of 15 minutes, you know one of three things:

**KEEP GOING.** Everything is on track. The constraint is right. The tasks are working. The direction is correct. Do the same thing next week.

**ADJUST.** The direction is right but the tasks need tweaking. Maria adjusted: same goal, different target companies. This is Module 5-6 territory — revise the plan, not the decision.

**BACKTRACK.** Something fundamental changed. The constraint might be wrong. The decision might need revision. Go back to Module 4 or 5.

Most weeks are "keep going." Occasionally "adjust." Rarely "backtrack."

The review is what prevents you from executing perfectly in the wrong direction for months.

*When is YOUR Sunday review? Pick a time. Put it in your calendar. Same time every week.*

# Your Body Already Knows

## I spent months fixing my sleep. The fix took one decision.

I stopped sleeping in March.

Not completely. The kind where you close your eyes at midnight and open them at 5am feeling like you ran a marathon in your dreams.

I went to the doctor. Blood work: fine. Heart: fine.

"Maybe try magnesium," she said.

So I tried magnesium. Then melatonin. Then no screens after 8pm. A weighted blanket. A $200 pillow that promised "cervical alignment."

Still couldn't sleep.

You know what fixed it?

I quit my job.

Not the magnesium. Not the pillow.

**A decision.**

The insomnia stopped in three days. Not gradually. Three days.

## Your body talks before your mind admits anything

I'm not a doctor. I'm not going to tell you that every headache is a decision you're avoiding.

Sometimes you're just sick.

But I noticed something in myself that I've now seen in dozens of friends. The pattern is too consistent to be coincidence:

- Before I admitted I needed to leave my job — **insomnia for months**
- Before a friend admitted her relationship was over — **six months of stomach problems.** They disappeared after she moved out.
- Before another friend admitted he hated his career — **chronic back pain** that no physiotherapist could explain

The body doesn't debate. It doesn't journal. It doesn't take a workshop.

It just shows you.

## How the volume gets louder

It starts quiet.

A low hum of anxiety you can't quite name. Something feels off but you can't point to it.

Then it gets louder:
- Sleep gets worse
- You're irritable with people who don't deserve it
- Sunday nights feel heavy

Then louder still:
- The anxiety becomes a pattern
- Weight creeps up
- You lose interest in things you used to love
- You're tired in a way that rest doesn't fix

Your body is turning up the volume.

Not because something is medically wrong. Because there's a decision you're not making.

**And the longer you wait, the louder it gets.**

Here's a question to sit with:

*Is there a physical symptom in your life that showed up around the same time you started avoiding something?*

## The three currencies you're spending every day

The tricky thing about not deciding is that it feels free.

It's not.

You're paying every single day. In three ways that don't show up on a bank statement.

### 1. Time

My friend Ana spent 26 months thinking about leaving her job. I know because she counted.

Twenty-six months.

Not planning. Not researching alternatives. Thinking. Circling the same questions. "Should I?" "What if?" "Maybe after the next review."

Twenty-six months produced zero clarity.

Just twenty-six months of being twenty-six months closer to regretting it.

### 2. Energy

You know how your phone dies faster when too many apps are running in the background?

That's you.

Every unmade decision is an open app. It runs quietly all day, draining your battery. You're tired at 2pm and you blame it on sleep, or diet, or getting older.

It's not sleep. It's not diet.

**It's the three unresolved decisions running in the background.**

That's why you're exhausted in a way that rest doesn't fix.

### 3. The life you're not living

This one's invisible. Which makes it the most expensive.

When you make a wrong decision, you can see the damage. You can learn from it. You can fix it.

When you make no decision?

Nothing visible happens. Same place. Same routine. Looks fine from the outside.

But every day in the current situation is a day you're NOT in the next one. The one you actually want.

Add it up:
- Three years of "I should change careers"
- Two years of "I should have that conversation"
- One year of "I should start that thing"

**That's six years of a life spent preparing instead of living.**

## The moment the math flips

So when does someone actually decide?

I've watched this in myself. In Indy. In every friend who eventually moved.

It's not inspiration. It's not a vision board. It's not a workshop breakthrough.

**It's the moment when being stuck hurts more than the fear of doing the thing.**

That's it.

The math flips. Pain of staying becomes worse than fear of moving.

For me it was the insomnia. Months of bad sleep until I just couldn't do it anymore. My body forced the math to flip.

For Ana it was a panic attack in a parking lot before work. Her body, saying: *enough.*

## You're already past the inflection point

If you're reading this right now, the math has probably already flipped for you.

Otherwise you'd be watching Netflix, not taking a course about decisions.

The question isn't whether to decide. You already know you need to.

The question is: *what exactly should you decide?* And what does a decision actually look like?

That's the next class. The one thing that sits at the center of everything in this course.

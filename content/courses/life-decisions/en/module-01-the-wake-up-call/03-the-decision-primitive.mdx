# The Decision Primitive

## Three years of therapy. One phone call to a lawyer.

My friend Camila wanted a divorce. She'd known for a long time. Maybe years.

She read every book about relationships. She did couples therapy. Solo therapy. A retreat about "reconnecting with your feminine power."

She understood her marriage perfectly. The dynamics. The patterns. The wounds. She had a vocabulary for everything that was wrong.

Nothing changed.

Then one Tuesday afternoon she called a lawyer. The conversation lasted 14 minutes.

Everything before that phone call was noise.

Everything after it was motion. The conversation with her kids. The apartment search. The paperwork.

**Three years of understanding. One phone call of deciding.**

## A wish is not a decision

Let me show you what Camila actually did, because it's easy to miss.

"I want a divorce."

She'd been saying that for years. To herself. In her journal. To her therapist.

But "I want a divorce" is not a decision.

It's a wish. A direction. A feeling.

"I will call a lawyer by Friday."

*That* is a decision.

What's the difference?

**A wish has no verb that happens on a specific day.**

**A decision does.**

Camila had the wish for three years. The decision took one Tuesday.

## Try it right now

Think about the thing you've been "wanting" for a while.

| What you say | What it actually is | What the decision would be |
|---|---|---|
| "I want to leave my job" | A wish | "I will update my resume this weekend and apply to 3 companies by the end of the month" |
| "I want to be healthier" | An aspiration | "I will go to the gym Monday, Wednesday, and Friday this week" |
| "I want to start a business" | A dream | "I will register the company name this week and buy the domain" |

See the pattern?

Wishes live in the future tense. They're comfortable. You can carry them around for years and they don't ask anything of you.

Decisions live in the present. They have a deadline. They either happen or they don't.

**That's why you probably have twenty wishes and zero decisions.**

## The loop: where the decision sits

This entire course is built around one loop. I'll show you the whole shape now, and we'll spend the rest of the course going through it step by step.

It starts with discomfort. Your body sends a signal. The insomnia, the anxiety, the heaviness we talked about last class.

Then you get honest about where you actually are. Not the story you tell people. The real numbers.

Then you get clear about where you want to be. Not a fantasy. A specific picture of a life you'd actually want to live.

Then you find the one thing in the way. The one obstacle that, if you removed it, would change everything else. Camila's was her marriage. Not her job, not her health, not her finances. The marriage. Everything else was secondary.

Then — right in the center — **you decide.**

Not a wish. Not a plan. A commitment with a specific action, a date, and at least one person who knows about it.

Then you break it into daily actions. You do them. You check what's working. You adjust.

And eventually, the constraint is resolved. That specific pain is gone.

Then something interesting happens. A new discomfort surfaces. Something you couldn't see before because the old problem was blocking the view.

And the loop starts again.

## Why Camila's second time was faster

After the divorce, Camila's next big decision was about her career. Seven years at the same company.

But this time? Weeks, not years.

She already knew how to get honest with herself. She'd done it with the marriage.

She already knew how to find the real obstacle. She'd learned to look past the comfortable problems.

She already knew what a decision looked like:
- A verb
- A date
- A phone call

The second time through the loop, she was faster. Not because the problem was easier. Because **she had done it before.**

By the third time — her finances — she did it in days.

> The skill of deciding gets better with practice. Like any skill. The first rep is the hardest. Each one after is a little faster.

## Three ideas to carry forward

I'm not going to list nine principles. You'd forget them by tomorrow.

Instead, remember three things from Camila's story:

**1. Nothing changed until she called the lawyer.**

Not the books. Not the therapy. Not the retreat. The phone call. The decision is the thing that makes everything else move.

**2. Not deciding was the most expensive option.**

She waited three years. Those years cost her time, energy, and the life she's living now. Every day of delay was a day of the better life she wasn't living.

**3. The second time was faster.**

The loop compounds. Each decision makes the next one easier. That's what this course builds. Not one dramatic breakthrough. A system that gets better every time you use it.

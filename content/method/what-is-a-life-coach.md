A life coach is a professional who helps people clarify goals, overcome obstacles, and make changes in their personal or professional lives. The International Coaching Federation reports over 71,000 coach practitioners worldwide, generating $2.85 billion annually. It's one of the fastest-growing industries in the world. And it's almost completely unregulated.

That last part matters.

## What Life Coaches Actually Do

Here's the typical coaching process. You book a session — usually 45 to 60 minutes, over Zoom or in person. The coach asks you questions. Big, open-ended ones. "What do you want?" "What's stopping you?" "What would you do if you couldn't fail?"

You talk. They listen. Sometimes they reflect back what you said in a way that makes you see it differently. Sometimes they challenge you. Most of the time, they hold you accountable — "Did you do the thing we talked about last week?"

There are different types. Executive coaches focus on career and leadership. Wellness coaches focus on health. Relationship coaches, money coaches, spiritual coaches. The ICF has a credentialing process, but it's voluntary. Anyone can call themselves a life coach tomorrow and start charging $200 an hour.

The good ones are genuinely skilled at asking the right questions at the right time. The mediocre ones are expensive mirrors. The bad ones give advice they're not qualified to give.

## What It Costs

Most life coaches charge between $150 and $500 per session. Some charge more. The typical engagement is weekly sessions for 3-12 months.

Let's do the math. At $250 per session, weekly for 6 months, that's $6,500. For a year, $13,000. Some high-end coaches charge $1,000+ per session for corporate clients.

That's a meaningful investment. The question is: what are you buying?

## Coach vs. Therapist vs. Self-Help vs. Methodology

These four things get confused constantly. Here's the honest breakdown.

**Therapists** are licensed. They can diagnose. They often explore your past to understand your present. If you have clinical depression, anxiety, PTSD, or unresolved trauma — see a therapist. No life coach should be touching that.

**Life coaches** are unlicensed. They focus on the future. They ask questions and hold you accountable. The good ones help you see blind spots. But they can't treat mental health conditions, and some don't know where that line is.

**Self-help** — books, courses, podcasts — gives you information. Lots of it. The problem isn't the information. The problem is that [information doesn't produce decisions](/concepts/self-help-doesnt-work). You can read 50 books on how to get unstuck and still be sitting in the same chair.

**A methodology** is different from all three. It's not a person. It's not information. It's a system — a structured process that takes you from stuck to decided to doing. You run it yourself. It asks you the hard questions, forces you to answer them, and decomposes your decision into actions you can take today.

## When Coaching Doesn't Work

Coaching fails when the problem isn't knowledge or accountability — it's the decision itself.

Think about it. You hire a coach because you [feel stuck](/concepts/feeling-stuck-in-life). You do the sessions. You have breakthroughs. You write goals. You make vision boards. You feel inspired after every session.

And then you go home and do nothing.

Not because you're lazy. Not because the coach is bad. But because the thing you need to do requires a decision you haven't made. The coach can't make that decision for you. Nobody can. That's your job.

Here's what I've noticed. The people who get the most out of coaching are the ones who've already decided and need help executing. The people who get the least out of it are the ones who are still hoping someone else will tell them what to do.

If you're in the second group, no amount of coaching sessions will move you. You don't need more accountability. You need to decide.

## The Dependency Problem

There's something nobody in the coaching industry likes to talk about. The business model creates dependency.

A coach gets paid per session. More sessions mean more revenue. There's a structural incentive to keep you coming back. Not malicious — most coaches genuinely want to help. But the incentive exists.

The best coaching is the kind that makes itself unnecessary. You show up, you do the work, you learn the framework, and you leave. Six months, maybe twelve. Then you fly solo.

But that's not how most coaching works. Most coaching becomes a standing appointment. Like therapy that never ends, except without the clinical justification.

Ask yourself: am I learning a skill, or am I renting a person?

## The Alternative

What if you could get the structured questioning of a good coach, the accountability of regular sessions, and the framework of a proven methodology — without the $13,000/year price tag?

That's what a methodology-based approach gives you. Instead of paying someone to ask you questions, you learn to ask them yourself. Instead of waiting for next Thursday's session to report progress, you have a daily system that keeps you moving.

The Right Decision methodology works like this: you map where you are, define where you want to be, find the one decision standing in the way, make it, decompose it into daily actions, and execute. Each cycle gets faster. Each decision gets easier.

The difference isn't motivation. It's structure. A coach gives you a person. A methodology gives you a skill.

And skills don't charge by the hour.

## The Honest Answer

Do you need a life coach? Maybe. If you've already made the decision and need help with execution, a good coach can accelerate your progress. If you're dealing with mental health issues, see a therapist first.

But if you're [stuck because you haven't decided](/concepts/how-to-make-big-life-decisions) — if you keep going in circles, doing everything except the one thing that would actually change your life — a coach isn't what you need.

You need to decide. And then you need a system that turns that decision into action, every single day, until the decision is done.

That's not coaching. That's a methodology.

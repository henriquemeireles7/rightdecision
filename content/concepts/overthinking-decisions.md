Overthinking decisions is the habit of repeatedly analyzing a choice without moving toward commitment. Unlike careful deliberation — which has a timeline and criteria — overthinking has no endpoint. It cycles through the same considerations indefinitely, generating more doubt with each pass. Psychologists call this rumination, and research consistently links it to increased anxiety, depression, and decreased life satisfaction.

## Overthinking is a decision you don't recognize

Here's the thing nobody tells you about overthinking: it is itself a decision.

When you "can't decide" whether to quit your job, you've decided to stay. When you "need more time" to figure out whether to end a relationship, you've decided to remain in it. When you're "not ready" to start the business, you've decided not to start.

Not deciding is always a decision. It's just the one with the worst returns.

In the Right Decision methodology, we call this Sin #8: not deciding and pretending that's not a decision. The default is always a choice — and it's almost always the most expensive one.

## Why overthinking feels productive

Overthinking hijacks the same neural pathways as actual problem-solving. When you're running through scenarios in your head — "if I do X, then Y might happen, but if Z..." — your brain registers this as useful work. You feel engaged. You feel like you're getting closer to the answer.

You're not. You're running on a treadmill. The scenery isn't changing.

The difference between thinking and overthinking is this: thinking produces a decision. Overthinking produces more thinking.

If you've been "thinking about it" for more than two weeks and you don't have a clear commitment with a deadline, you're not thinking. You're avoiding. The distinction matters because the remedy is different. Thinking needs more data. Avoiding needs a deadline.

## The invisible cost

You can see the cost of a bad decision. You took the job and hated it. You moved to the city and it wasn't right. You invested and lost money. These costs are visible, countable, story-worthy.

You cannot see the cost of no decision. The career you didn't start. The three years of salary difference. The relationship you didn't pursue. The health you didn't prioritize. These don't show up in your story because they didn't happen.

But they compound.

Calculate it. How long have you been thinking about the thing? Six months? A year? Three years? What has that delay cost you — in money, in time, in energy, in opportunity? The number is almost always shocking.

One woman calculated that three years of indecision about a career change cost her approximately $180K in the salary differential she didn't earn. She'd spent more time agonizing about the decision than it would have taken to make the change, hate it, and change again.

## How to stop

**1. Name the decision.** Not "I need to figure out my life." That's not a decision. "I need to decide whether to apply for management positions by Friday." That's a decision. Precision kills overthinking because it gives your brain a specific target instead of an infinite search space.

**2. Set a deadline that scares you a little.** Not six months. Not "when I'm ready." This week. Friday. The discomfort you feel is not a sign that you need more time. It's a sign that the decision matters.

**3. Accept 70% confidence.** You will never have 100% certainty about a big decision. If you did, it wouldn't be a decision — it would be arithmetic. 70% confidence is enough to act. The remaining 30% reveals itself through action, not analysis.

**4. Separate deciding from planning from doing.** Overthinking collapses three distinct steps into one impossible task. Today: decide. Tomorrow: plan. Day after: start. Each step is manageable alone. Together, they're overwhelming.

**5. Ask the right question.** Not "what if I'm wrong?" but "what does it cost me to not decide for another month?" When you compare the cost of a wrong decision against the cost of [continued indecision](/concepts/feeling-stuck-in-life), the math usually favors action.

The people who make good [life decisions](/concepts/how-to-make-big-life-decisions) aren't the ones who think the longest. They're the ones who decide the fastest — not recklessly, but with structure. They're running a [system](/concepts/how-to-make-big-life-decisions), not relying on willpower.

You already know what you need to do. You've known for a while. The only thing missing is the decision.

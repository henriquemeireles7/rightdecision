Decision fatigue is the deterioration of decision quality after a long session of decision-making. Research by social psychologist Roy Baumeister demonstrated that willpower and decision-making draw from the same limited mental resource — when you deplete one, the other suffers. This is why you can crush it at work all day and then eat garbage for dinner. Your brain ran out of decision fuel.

## What decision fatigue actually looks like

It's 6pm. You've been productive all day. Meetings, emails, project decisions, team management. Now your partner asks: "What do you want for dinner?"

And you snap. Or you say "I don't care, you pick." Or you order the same thing you always order. Not because you lack preferences — because your brain has been making decisions for 10 hours straight and it's done.

This is the mild version. The dangerous version happens with bigger decisions.

You've been researching apartments for three months. You've seen 15 places. Each one requires comparing price, location, size, commute time, neighborhood safety, natural light, pet policy. By apartment number 12, you're not comparing anymore. You're just picking whichever one looks the least exhausting to think about.

Decision fatigue doesn't make you stupid. It makes you take shortcuts. You start choosing the default, the easiest option, or nothing at all. The quality of your choices degrades silently — you feel like you're still being rational, but you're running on fumes.

## Why it matters for life decisions

Here's the problem: life decisions don't wait for you to be fresh.

The decision to leave a job, end a relationship, move cities, start a business — these decisions sit in the background while you use up your daily decision budget on email replies and grocery choices. By the time you get to the decision that actually matters, you're depleted. So you default to the status quo.

"I'll think about it next week."

Next week, same thing. The big decision never gets the fresh brain it deserves because the small decisions ate all the bandwidth.

In the Right Decision methodology, this is why the Decision Cycle starts with diagnosis, not action. Before you can make a good decision, you need to identify which decision matters most — your dominant constraint. Then you protect your decision energy for that one thing.

## How to beat decision fatigue

**1. Make your most important decision first.** Before email. Before meetings. Before the day has a chance to drain you. If you're deciding whether to quit your job, do that thinking at 7am on a Saturday with coffee, not at 9pm on a Wednesday after a full workday.

**2. Reduce trivial decisions.** This is why Steve Jobs wore the same outfit. Not because he was eccentric — because he understood that every trivial decision costs the same mental resource as an important one. Create defaults: meal prep, standard outfits, automated bills, recurring calendar blocks.

**3. Batch similar decisions.** Don't make financial decisions scattered across the week. Set a "money hour" where you handle all financial choices at once. Same for career decisions, health decisions, relationship decisions. Batching reduces the switching cost between different types of thinking.

**4. Use a system, not willpower.** A decision-making system — like the [Decision Cycle](/concepts/how-to-make-big-life-decisions) — means you don't start from scratch every time. The system tells you what to evaluate, in what order, with what criteria. You're not fighting [analysis paralysis](/concepts/analysis-paralysis) and decision fatigue at the same time.

**5. Recognize when you're depleted.** If you notice yourself defaulting to "whatever" or "I'll decide later" — stop. That's decision fatigue talking. Pause, rest, and come back to the decision when you're fresh. A delayed decision made well is better than a depleted decision made now.

The cost of ignoring decision fatigue is invisible but real. It's not the wrong decision you make — it's the right decision you keep [avoiding because you're too tired to face it](/concepts/feeling-stuck-in-life).

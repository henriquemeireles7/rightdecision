I had multiple companies. Exits. Money. The stuff people point to when they say "he's doing fine."

Then I was almost unemployed for a year in the US.

I was doing everything. Therapy. Meditation. Reading books. Morning routines. Planning sessions. Journaling. Vision boards. I had all the tools. Every single one that the self-development industry sells you.

Nothing was moving.

Not because the tools were wrong. They weren't. Therapy helped me understand things I couldn't see before. Meditation calmed me down. The books were genuinely good. But understanding, calm, and knowledge were not producing change. I was becoming the most self-aware stuck person I knew.

Then I stopped. All of it.

I stopped the therapy. Stopped the meditation. Stopped the morning routines and the evening reflections and the weekly reviews. I just went to the computer and started doing things that made me excited. That's it.

I woke up, did the thing, and went to sleep. No framework. No plan. No vision board. Just the thing.

## What happened next surprised me

After a few weeks, clarity arrived. Not before the action. After.

This is the part nobody talks about. Every self-help guru says "get clarity first, then act." They're wrong. Or at least, they're incomplete. Clarity doesn't come from thinking. It comes from doing. You can sit in a room and think about your life for a year and you will not be clearer at the end. You'll just be more comfortable with being unclear.

It was through doing that everything became clear. The theories I had been juggling for months collapsed into one insight: the decision was the thing. Not the information. Not the motivation. Not the understanding. The decision to do it, and then doing it.

I started noticing it everywhere. The friends who were doing well? They weren't smarter. They hadn't read more books. They just decided faster. They made the call, dealt with the consequences, adjusted, and moved on. The friends who were stuck? They had beautiful plans. Perfect diagnoses. Zero decisions.

I felt like maybe decision is the universal primitive of human beings. We are here to make decisions. Depression and not making decisions are related. Anxiety and making bad decisions are related. Everything in life could be linked to decisions.

But it took me years to make one right decision. I did it by luck, by trying too many times until luck found me.

That didn't sit right. If decisions are so fundamental, why don't we have a system for making them? We have systems for everything else — financial planning, workout routines, project management. But for the thing that matters most? "Trust your gut." "Follow your heart." "The answer will come."

No. The answer won't come. You make the answer.

## Indy saw the same thing from the other side

My wife Indy went through the same process — different path, same conclusion. She watched friends spend thousands on coaches, therapists, and courses. She watched them become fluent in the language of their pain without ever doing anything about it.

"They know exactly what's wrong," she told me once. "They can explain their patterns, name their attachment styles, draw their trauma map. And they're still in the same place."

She has this test. If content sounds like a coach on Instagram — rewrite it. If it sounds like a woman who just finished the laundry and is telling you the truth — ship it. We call it the Indy Test, and everything we publish has to pass it.

## The thing nobody wants to say out loud

Here's what we noticed. Every self-help program, every therapy protocol, every coaching framework does the same thing: it helps you understand the past.

Therapy gives you language for your pain. Energy work gives you chakra alignment. Manifestation gives you vision boards. Coaching gives you someone else's diagnosis. They all point backward or upward. None of them point forward. None of them ask: "What are you going to do about it? Specifically? By when?"

That question is uncomfortable. Which is exactly why nobody asks it.

Because the business model works better without it. A customer who understands but doesn't act is a customer who comes back for more understanding. The entire industry — and we include therapy here, not just the Instagram coaches — is built on a loop: consume, process, feel better, get hit by life, repeat.

Think about it. How much have you spent trying to get unstuck? Therapy at $150-300 per session. Coaching at $200-500 per hour. Online courses at $47 to $997 each. Retreats at $1,500 and up. Over the last three years, what did all of that produce?

Understanding. Insights. Language about your pain.

How many decisions did it produce?

We're not saying understanding is worthless. We're saying it's not sufficient. And the industry that sells it pretends it is. Understanding without action is the most expensive form of procrastination.

If you've ever felt like you were [doing everything right and nothing was changing](/concepts/overthinking-decisions) — that's why. You were stuck in the loop. Not because you're broken. Because the loop is designed to feel like progress.

## So we built the thing we wished we'd had

We got tired of recommending other people's programs to our friends — and watching those friends stay stuck. We were making other creators money while our friends stayed in the same place.

So we built The Right Decision.

It's built on one idea: you are stuck because you're not making decisions, not because you lack understanding.

The methodology is a cycle, and it starts where you actually are. Not where you tell people you are. Not where your Instagram suggests you are. Where you actually are — financially, emotionally, professionally, relationally. Honest numbers. Real facts. No stories.

Then you define where you want to be. Not a metric — "$50K per month" is not a destination, it's a number. A condition of life. "Freedom to choose how I spend my time." That's a destination.

Then you find the one thing in the way. Not the loudest thing. Not the easiest thing. The single biggest constraint between here and there. This is the step most people skip, and it's the step that matters most. The [dominant constraint](/concepts/how-to-make-big-life-decisions) is usually the thing you're avoiding.

Then you decide. Name it. Write it down. Tell someone. Make it concrete enough that you can't sleepwalk back to the default.

Then you break it into daily actions and do the thing. One task per day. Not motivation. Not willpower. Just the next thing on the list. After a few weeks, clarity arrives. It was through action that everything became clear.

Then you re-evaluate. The constraint shifts. A new cycle begins. Each one faster because you've done it before.

We paired the methodology with AI skills that ask you better questions than you'd ask yourself. Not a chatbot. Not generic prompts. A structured system that follows the methodology, personalized to your situation. The AI doesn't tell you what to decide. It asks the right questions until you see it yourself.

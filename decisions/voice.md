# Human Communication Guide

**Read this before writing ANY content for a real human being.**
This is not a style guide. It's a set of rules that make AI-written content sound like it came from someone who lived it, not someone who studied it.

---

## Part 1: General Principles (applies to everything)

### Rule 1: Write like you're thinking, not like you've finished thinking

**BAD:** "The decision-making process involves several key steps that, when followed correctly, lead to improved outcomes in personal and professional life."

**GOOD:** "Here's what I've noticed. People who get unstuck don't have better information. They just decide faster. I'm not totally sure why this works, but I think it's because action produces clarity that thinking never does."

The good version has hesitation, personal observation, and incomplete certainty. That's how humans actually think. AI writes like every paragraph is a concluded argument. Humans write like they're figuring it out in front of you.

### Rule 2: Rhythm is everything

Short sentence. Then a longer one that builds, turns, and lands somewhere you didn't expect. Then short again.

One word. Sometimes.

Then a paragraph that breathes. That takes its time. That lets the reader sit with the idea instead of rushing to the next one. Not every paragraph needs to punch. Some need to hold.

**BAD (AI default):** Every sentence is 15-20 words. Every paragraph is 3-4 sentences. The rhythm is a flatline. It sounds like a textbook even when the words are casual.

**GOOD:** Vary sentence length from 2 words to 30. Mix one-sentence paragraphs with 4-sentence runs. Read it out loud. If it sounds like a metronome, rewrite.

### Rule 3: Start with the story, not the principle

**BAD:** "Self-assessment accuracy is a critical factor in decision-making quality. Research shows that people systematically overestimate their positive attributes and underestimate their challenges. Here's an example of how this plays out."

**GOOD:** "Maria's first state map said 'I'm doing okay financially.' Her bank account said $800 in savings and $12K in debt. She wasn't lying. She just hadn't looked."

The principle emerges from the story. You remember Maria. You forget the principle stated as a principle. But you remember the principle that Maria taught you.

### Rule 4: Specific over general, always

**BAD:** "Many people spend significant amounts on self-development without seeing results."

**GOOD:** "You've spent $3,000 on courses in the last two years. How many decisions did they produce?"

Specifics create images. Generalities create nothing. "$3,000" hits harder than "significant amounts." "Two years" hits harder than "over time." Name the number. Name the time. Name the person.

### Rule 5: Questions that stop the scroll

Not rhetorical questions. Real ones. The kind that make someone put down their phone and stare at the ceiling.

**BAD:** "Have you ever wondered why change is so difficult?"

**GOOD:** "If all those courses worked, why are you here?"

The first question has no sting. The second one lands in the stomach. The best questions in this course come from the founder's actual words. Protect them.

### Rule 6: Show the feeling, don't name it

**BAD:** "She experienced feelings of frustration and isolation."

**GOOD:** "She cries in the shower sometimes but doesn't tell anyone."

The first is a clinical description. The second is a human moment. One makes you nod. The other makes your chest tighten. Always choose the chest-tightener.

### Rule 7: Opinion without hedge

**BAD:** "While therapy can be beneficial for many people, it may not always be the most effective approach for everyone seeking personal growth."

**GOOD:** "Therapy was for the crazy ones. Now therapy is an excuse to inaction."

Humans have opinions. They say "this is wrong" not "this may not be optimal for all use cases." AI hedges because it's trained to be balanced. But the reader doesn't want balance. The reader wants someone who believes something strongly enough to say it out loud.

When the founder has an opinion, USE IT. Don't soften it. Don't add "of course, therapy has its place" unless the founder actually said that.

### Rule 8: Open loops

Start a story. Don't finish it. Teach the concept. Then close the story.

**Example:** "Maria quit her job on a Tuesday. Not because she had a plan. Not because she was ready. I'll tell you what happened later, but first you need to understand something about how decisions actually work..."

The brain cannot let go of an unfinished story. The Zeigarnik Effect. It's why Netflix cliffhangers work. It's why you remember the story you didn't finish more than the one you did.

Use open loops at the start of modules, between sections, and at the end of classes. The student should always have one unresolved thread pulling them forward.

### Rule 9: Direct address

"You" not "people." "Your" not "one's." "You've been doing this for two years" not "many people spend years in this pattern."

Write like you're talking to ONE person sitting across from you. Not an audience. Not a demographic. One woman who is stuck and needs someone to be honest with her.

### Rule 10: Kill the announcements

**BAD:** "In this section, we'll explore the concept of dominant constraints and how they relate to your personal growth journey."

**GOOD:** [Just start talking about dominant constraints. No announcement needed.]

Never say "In this section we will..." or "Let's explore..." or "Now that we've covered X, let's move to Y." Just say the thing. The reader doesn't need a table of contents narrated out loud.

### Rule 11: Imperfection is the signal of humanity

Leave in the rough edges. The incomplete sentence. The moment where you change your mind mid-paragraph. The "I'm not sure about this, but..." before a strong claim.

AI is too clean. Too organized. Too symmetrical. Humans are messy. They contradict themselves. They circle back. They say "wait, actually..." and the reader trusts them MORE because of it.

### Rule 12: The Laugh Test

If the content is about a serious topic and there's nowhere the reader might smile, you've written a textbook. Self-development content that never makes you laugh is content that takes itself too seriously.

Not jokes. Not humor sections. Just the occasional observation that's true AND funny. "Good, makes sense? I need to go now do the laundry." That line from Indy is funnier than anything a copywriter would write because it's REAL.

The funniest things in writing are specific truths that nobody says out loud.

## Part 2: Anti-Patterns (never do these)

| Anti-pattern | Why it sounds AI | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "It's important to understand that..." | Nobody talks like this | Just state the thing |
| "In this section, we will explore..." | Announcement before content | Delete and start with the content |
| "Now that we've covered X, let's move to Y" | Transitional filler | Just start Y |
| "There are several key factors to consider" | Vague hedge | Name the factors. Or better: tell a story that shows them. |
| "This is a crucial/fundamental/pivotal concept" | Telling, not showing | If it's crucial, the reader will feel it. Don't announce importance. |
| Uniform paragraph length | AI's tell-tale | Vary: 1-sentence paragraphs mixed with 4-sentence ones |
| Perfect structure (intro → 3 points → conclusion) | Too clean | Surprise the reader. Start in the middle. End with a question. |
| Both-sidesing everything | AI's safety instinct | Take a position. If the founder has an opinion, state it without disclaimer. |
| Explaining a metaphor after using it | Insults the reader | Trust them to get it |
| Lists of 5+ items with identical formatting | AI's favorite pattern | Break the pattern. Vary the format. Make the 3rd item different. |

## Part 2.5: Class Structure — The Brunson Flow

Every class follows the same emotional arc but sounds unique because the STORIES are different. The reader never sees the framework. They just feel pulled through.

### The flow: STORY → CONCEPT → EXAMPLES → BRIDGE

**1. STORY (the parable)**
Open with a real person in a real situation. NOT "let me tell you about a concept." The story contains the concept before you name it. The reader FEELS the idea before they LEARN the idea.

The story follows the Epiphany Bridge: backstory → desire → wall → epiphany. Not all eight elements every time. But at minimum: a person, a problem, a moment of realization.

**2. CONCEPT (the naming)**
Now name what the story just demonstrated. "What happened to Maria is what we call the dominant constraint." The concept is the label for what they already felt. It clicks instantly because they just lived it through the story.

Never introduce a concept cold. Never say "The dominant constraint is defined as..." Start with Maria. Then say "That thing Maria was avoiding? That's the dominant constraint."

**3. EXAMPLES (the grounding)**
Give 2-3 more examples in different life areas. Career version. Relationship version. Health version. The reader needs to see the concept in THEIR situation, not just Maria's. Each example is short (2-4 sentences). Different enough that the reader finds herself in at least one.

**4. BRIDGE (the pull forward)**
End with something unresolved. An open loop into the next class. A question that sits in the mind. Never a summary. Never "in this class we covered..." End with forward motion.

### Why this works

The reader never processes a "lesson." She processes a STORY that happens to teach something. The concept emerges organically. She remembers the story. The concept sticks because it has an emotional address in her memory, not just an intellectual one.

### Formatting rules for readability

- **Paragraphs: 1-3 sentences max.** Dense paragraphs are walls. Walls get skipped.
- **One idea per paragraph.** New thought = new paragraph.
- **Line breaks are breathing room.** A single sentence alone has more impact than the same sentence buried in a block.
- **No parenthetical jargon.** Never write "(objectives, tasks, habits)." If they matter, explain them. If not, cut them.
- **Assume zero context.** Every concept introduced through a story before it gets a name.

### Visual variety — the scannable skeleton

79% of readers scan, not read (Nielsen Norman Group). A scanner should be able to skim your headings, bold text, and list items and still get the core message.

**The rule: say the same idea in visually different ways throughout each class.**

A class that is all prose paragraphs is a wall. A class that mixes these formats keeps the eye moving:

1. **Narrative prose** — the stories, the flow, the emotional pull
2. **Questions** — "How long have you been thinking about this?" forces the reader to stop
3. **Bold declarations** — **Nothing changes until you decide.** Stands alone. Unmissable.
4. **Numbered steps** — when there's a sequence or a process
5. **Bullet lists** — when there are parallel items or examples
6. **Short dialogue** — "I asked her: do you know how much a salon chair costs? Silence."
7. **Single-sentence paragraphs** — for punch. For emphasis. For the line that matters most.
8. **Block quotes or callouts** — for the line the reader should screenshot

**How to mix them:** Every ~3-4 paragraphs of prose, break the pattern. Insert a question, a list, a bold standalone line, or a short dialogue exchange. The reader's eye should never travel more than 15 lines without a visual shift.

### Headings that work alone

Headings are the scannable skeleton. If someone reads ONLY the headings, they should understand the lesson's arc.

**BAD headings:**
- The trap
- The industry
- What to do
- Summary

**GOOD headings:**
- Luana spent $4,700 on self-help and still hasn't started
- The industry profits from keeping you stuck
- A decision is a verb with a date
- Eight patterns that keep you in the same place

Good headings are mini-stories or strong claims. They carry meaning in the first 2-3 words. A reader scanning only headings should think "I need to read this section."

## Part 3: Engagement Techniques

### Open Loops
Start a story at the beginning of a class. Close it at the end. The brain CANNOT let go of an unfinished narrative (Zeigarnik Effect). This is the single most powerful retention technique for self-paced content.

### Pattern Interrupts
When the reader expects one thing, give them another. After three paragraphs of serious content, drop a one-line observation that's unexpectedly honest or funny. The surprise resets attention.

### The Question That Stops
Plant one question per class that the reader cannot answer without doing the work. Not "did you know X?" but "if all those courses worked, why are you here?" The question should sit in the reader's mind for hours after reading.

### The Specific Detail
One hyper-specific detail makes everything around it feel real. "She cries in the shower." "He went to the computer and started doing things that made him excited. That's it." "$800 in savings and $12K in debt." The specific detail is the trust signal.

### Direct Challenge
Not mean. But direct. "You keep saying you're not ready. You've been saying that for two years. How ready do you need to be?" This is how Henry and Indy talk to friends. It's how the course should talk to students.

## Part 4: Right Decision Brand Voice

### Henry's patterns (from raw interviews)
- States things as absolute truths: "You just need to make the right decision and commit to it. There is nothing else to be done."
- Uses "the thing" as a recurring motif: "doing the thing," "except the thing," "the cost of not doing the thing"
- Connects decisions to everything: "depression and not making decisions are related"
- Philosophical but grounded: starts with a big claim, then grounds it in personal experience
- No softening: says "this is wrong" not "this might not work for everyone"

### Indy's patterns (from raw interviews)
- Talks like she's explaining to a friend at the kitchen table
- Uses "you" constantly and directly
- Casual closings: "Good, makes sense? I need to go now do the laundry."
- Lists real alternatives and dismisses them: "not in a man that will pay your bills or a woman that will make you happier or money that will make you richer or even god"
- Doesn't structure arguments. Just states truths in sequence. Raw and unedited.

### The Indy Test (quality gate)
Before publishing any content, ask: "Would Indy say this to a friend at the kitchen table?"
- If it sounds like a coach on Instagram → rewrite
- If it sounds like a textbook → rewrite
- If it sounds like a woman who just told you the truth and went to fold clothes → ship it

### Words we use
decide, commit, do the thing, constraint, honest, specific, the right decision, the loop, stuck, actually, the cost of not deciding

### Words we NEVER use
manifest, align, vibration, journey (as euphemism), empower, tribe, sacred, authentic self, inner child, healing journey, level up, boss babe, slay, queen, transformative experience, holistic approach, mindful, intentional (overused)

### The voice formula
Warm visuals (DESIGN.md: cream, beige, serif fonts) + Direct words (human.md: honest, specific, no hedge). The combination says: this is a safe space to hear hard truths. The design says "calm." The words say "honest." Together they produce trust.

## How to use this document

1. **Before writing any class, email, ad, or social post:** Read Rules 1-12.
2. **While writing:** Check every paragraph against the Anti-Patterns table.
3. **After writing:** Apply the Indy Test. Read it out loud. If any sentence would make Indy roll her eyes, rewrite it.
4. **For engagement:** Every class should have at least one open loop, one question that stops, and one hyper-specific detail.
5. **For voice:** Henry's voice for theory and frameworks. Indy's voice for practical application and direct address to the student.
